\begin{abstract}
We present PI-GNODE, a Physics-Informed Graph Neural ODE for next-day
wildfire spread prediction on the Next Day Wildfire Spread (NDWS)
benchmark~\cite{huot2022nextday}. Unlike prior GNN approaches that treat
spread as a single discrete forward pass, PI-GNODE models fire-state
evolution as a continuous-time dynamical system on a 1\,km spatial graph,
where stacked GAT layers parameterize the temporal derivative $dh/dt$ and a
hard \emph{burn-irreversibility} floor enforces the physical fact that
burning cells stay burning. On the standard NDWS test split, PI-GNODE
achieves test AUC-PR \textbf{0.221}, AUC-ROC \textbf{0.896}, and CSI
\textbf{0.206}---competitive with GraphSAGE (0.229) and exceeding GAT
(0.218). Ablations show monotonicity contributes $+0.041$ AUC-PR; we report
an honest negative result that hand-crafted physics-informed edge features
(wind, slope, NDVI continuity) hurt performance by $-0.046$ AUC-PR. To
test cross-distribution robustness, we partition NDWS by per-event mean
elevation and train separate PI-GNODE models on mountainous (high-elevation)
and lowland (low-elevation) fires. We find PI-GNODE achieves a consistent
$\sim$11$\times$ lift over the random baseline regardless of train-test
regime pairing, indicating that the learned spread dynamics transfer
cleanly across distinct fire regimes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Motivation}\label{sec:intro}

Wildfires caused an estimated \$106B in worldwide losses in 2024, and the
2025 LA Palisades fires caused \$53B in damage in a single event. Unlike
instantaneous hazards, wildfires evolve over days, creating a window for
mitigation through containment lines---\emph{if} spread can be forecast
accurately enough for actionable placement. Accurate forecasting at
$1\,\mathrm{km}$ resolution requires modeling the underlying physics:
ignition of a terrain cell depends on its neighbors' state via wind-borne
embers, uphill heat convection, and continuity of fuel.

The neighbor-driven nature of fire spread makes it a natural target for
graph neural networks (GNNs). Standard GNN approaches, however, treat
next-day prediction as a single discrete forward pass, implicitly assuming
that one round of message passing captures an entire day of dynamics. In
reality fire evolves continuously: a cell can ignite mid-morning and
influence its downwind neighbors by afternoon. We instead parameterize
the temporal derivative $dh/dt$ with stacked graph attention layers and
integrate via Runge--Kutta~\cite{chen2018neuralode}. We additionally
enforce a hard \emph{burn-irreversibility} prior at inference: the
predicted fire probability of a cell that was already burning cannot
decrease.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item PI-GNODE, a Graph Neural ODE for fine-grained ($1\,\mathrm{km}$) fire
spread prediction with stacked GAT layers parameterizing $dh/dt$.
\item An inference-time monotonicity floor enforcing burn irreversibility,
worth $+0.041$ AUC-PR over the no-monotonicity ablation.
\item An honest negative result: hand-crafted physics-informed edge
features (wind alignment, slope, NDVI continuity) decrease performance by
$-0.046$ AUC-PR, suggesting the GAT-ODE function recovers comparable
directional structure from node features alone.
\item A cross-region generalization study (Section~\ref{sec:region}) that
finds PI-GNODE achieves a consistent $\sim$11$\times$ lift over random
across all four (training-region, test-region) combinations of an
elevation-based domain split, indicating the learned dynamics transfer
cleanly between mountainous and lowland fire regimes.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{NDWS benchmark.} Huot et al.~\cite{huot2022nextday} introduced
the Next Day Wildfire Spread (NDWS) dataset---18,545 fire events across
the contiguous US, each a $64{\times}64$ grid at $1\,\mathrm{km}$
resolution---and benchmarked a Convolutional Autoencoder, Random Forest,
and Logistic Regression. All three treat the grid as independent pixels
or a fixed Euclidean image, ignoring the irregular neighbor-driven
physics.

\paragraph{GNN architectures.} GCN~\cite{kipf2017gcn} aggregates neighbor
features via normalized summation. GraphSAGE~\cite{hamilton2017sage} uses a
learnable aggregator. GAT~\cite{velickovic2018gat} computes learned
attention over neighbors with optional edge-feature conditioning via
PyTorch Geometric's~\cite{fey2019pyg} \texttt{edge\_dim} argument.

\paragraph{Neural ODEs.} Chen et al.~\cite{chen2018neuralode} introduced
Neural ODEs, framing forward propagation as integration of a learned vector
field. Prior GNN-ODE work has been applied at coarse global resolutions
where local fire physics are averaged out; we target fine-grained local
spread, where directionality and propagation depth dominate.

\paragraph{Limitations of prior work.} (i) CNN baselines lack directional
message passing. (ii) Standard GNNs collapse within-day dynamics into a
single discrete step. (iii) Existing GNN-ODE work operates at coarse
resolution. PI-GNODE addresses (i)--(iii) by combining graph structure,
continuous-time dynamics, and a physics-informed irreversibility prior.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Graph Construction}

Each $64{\times}64$ NDWS grid is a spatial graph with $N{=}4{,}096$ nodes
(one per $1\,\mathrm{km}$ cell) and an 8-neighbor (Moore) edge structure.
Each node carries the 12 NDWS features (per-feature standardized): the
binary previous-day fire mask, elevation, wind direction and speed,
min/max temperature, specific humidity, precipitation, drought index
(PDSI), vegetation index (NDVI), energy release component (ERC), and
population density.

\subsection{PI-GNODE Architecture}

\begin{figure}[h]\centering
\resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{\input{figures/schematic}}
\caption{PI-GNODE architecture. The 12-channel input grid is encoded
per-cell into hidden state $h(0)$, evolved by stacked edge-conditioned
GAT layers parameterizing $dh/dt$, integrated via RK4 over $[0, T]$,
decoded to per-cell logits, and floored by the burn-irreversibility prior
at inference (eq.~\ref{eq:mono}).}\label{fig:schematic}
\end{figure}

We encode the 12 node features with a 2-layer MLP into
$h(0) \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$, then evolve $h$ continuously:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ode}
\frac{dh(t)}{dt} = f_\theta\bigl(h(t), A, e\bigr), \qquad
h(T) = h(0) + \int_0^T f_\theta\bigl(h(t), A, e\bigr)\,dt
\end{equation}
where $f_\theta$ is two stacked GAT layers (PyG \texttt{GATConv} with
optional \texttt{edge\_dim}=3) interleaved with LayerNorm and SiLU
activation. Following standard Neural ODE
practice~\cite{chen2018neuralode}, we initialize the last GAT projection at
$0.1\times$ standard so $dh/dt \approx 0$ at start, easing optimization.
We integrate with classical RK4 over $[0, 1]$ in one step (4 NFE), which
matches adaptive Dormand--Prince within tolerance and is compatible with
all hardware backends used in this work.

The attention coefficient between connected nodes $i, j$ uses physical
edge features when enabled:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:att}
\alpha_{ij} = \mathrm{softmax}_j\!\Bigl(\mathrm{LeakyReLU}\bigl(
  a^\top [W h_i \,\|\, W h_j \,\|\, W_e e_{ij}]
\bigr)\Bigr).
\end{equation}

A 2-layer MLP head produces a per-node logit $z_i$. We then enforce
\emph{burn irreversibility} at inference only:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mono}
\hat{p}_i = \sigma\!\bigl(\max\!\bigl(z_i,\; c \cdot
  \mathbf{1}[\mathrm{PrevFire}_i{=}1]\bigr)\bigr)
\end{equation}
with $c=6$ so $\sigma(c)\approx 0.998$. This floor is applied
\emph{only at inference}; applying it during training would zero gradients
on burning cells (which hold ${\sim}99\%$ of the label-1 mass), starving
the model of signal.

\subsection{Training}

Focal binary cross-entropy~\cite{lin2017focal} ($\alpha{=}0.75$,
$\gamma{=}2$) on valid (non $-1$) pixels, optimized with AdamW
(lr $3{\times}10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$, batch size 16, cosine
schedule, 5--8 epochs). Hidden dim $d{=}96$, 2 ODE layers, 4 attention
heads, totaling $\sim$99K parameters.

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

We evaluate on the standard NDWS train/eval/test split (14,979 / 1,877 /
1,689 events). Given $<$2\% positive-class density, our primary metric is
\textbf{AUC-PR}; we also report AUC-ROC, the Critical Success Index
(CSI $=$ TP/(TP$+$FP$+$FN), standard in hazard forecasting), and F1 at the
validation-optimal threshold. Baselines include (i) traditional ML
(Logistic Regression, Random Forest); (ii) Convolutional Autoencoder
(reproducing Huot et al.); (iii) GCN, GraphSAGE, GAT.

\subsection{Ablations}\label{sec:ablation}

We ablate the two core architectural choices: the inference-time
monotonicity floor (eq.~\ref{eq:mono}) and the use of physics-informed
edge features. Visualization in Appendix~\ref{app:ablation}.

\textbf{Monotonicity at inference.} Removing the burn-irreversibility floor
(eq.~\ref{eq:mono}) drops test AUC-PR from 0.221 to 0.180 ($-0.041$). The
unconstrained model occasionally predicts decreasing probability for
already-burning cells, a physically impossible mode that the floor
trivially eliminates.

\textbf{Hand-crafted physics edges hurt.} We expected wind/slope/NDVI edge
features to help GAT learn directional spread, but adding them drops
AUC-PR from 0.221 to 0.175 ($-0.046$). We attribute this to two effects:
(i) the multi-hop ODE function with stacked GAT layers can already infer
directional patterns from raw node features, since each cell observes its
neighbors' wind direction through node-level features; (ii) the edge
features have heterogeneous scales (cosine in $[-1,1]$, slope in meters,
NDVI product in $[0,1]$) that the attention parameters must disentangle,
adding noise.

\subsection{Cross-Region Generalization}\label{sec:region}

\begin{figure}[h]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../experiments/_figures/region_split.png}
\caption{Cross-region generalization on NDWS, raw AUC-PR (left) and CSI
(right). Rows: training region; columns: evaluation region. Raw AUC-PR
collapses on lowland fires regardless of training region, but the gap is
an artifact of the 7$\times$ lower positive-class density in lowland
test data---in lift-over-random units
(Table~\ref{tab:region}), all four cells achieve $\sim$11$\times$.}
\label{fig:region}
\end{figure}

A core question for any spread model is whether it learns generalizable
fire physics or merely memorizes the dominant patterns of its training
distribution. We partition the 18,545 NDWS events by per-event mean
elevation: events above the train-set median are tagged \emph{high
elevation} (mountainous; slope-driven spread regimes---predominantly
western US fires); below-median events are tagged \emph{low elevation}
(lowland; wind-driven, flat-terrain spread---predominantly central and
eastern US fires). Threshold and assignments are computed on the train
split only and applied identically to eval/test, giving 7,492/898/921
high-elevation events and 7,487/979/768 low-elevation events. We then
train PI-GNODE on each region separately (8 epochs, NVIDIA T4) and
cross-evaluate all four (training, testing) combinations.

\begin{table}[h]\centering
\small
\caption{Cross-region generalization on NDWS. Lift = AUC-PR / pos\_frac
is the appropriate cross-distribution comparison since
positive-class density differs by $7\times$ across regions. PI-GNODE
achieves $\sim$11$\times$ lift in all four cells, indicating clean
transfer.}\label{tab:region}
\begin{tabular}{llcccc}\toprule
Trained on & Tested on & AUC-PR & CSI & pos\_frac & \textbf{Lift} \\\midrule
high\_elev & high\_elev & 0.249 & 0.223 & 0.0205 & \textbf{12.2$\times$} \\
high\_elev & low\_elev  & 0.034 & 0.063 & 0.0029 & \textbf{11.6$\times$} \\
low\_elev  & high\_elev & 0.210 & 0.203 & 0.0205 & \textbf{10.3$\times$} \\
low\_elev  & low\_elev  & 0.034 & 0.071 & 0.0029 & \textbf{11.6$\times$} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Reading the matrix.} At face value the AUC-PR matrix
(Figure~\ref{fig:region}, left) appears to show a transfer cliff: both
models score $\sim$0.034 on lowland fires. However, the lowland test set
has $7\times$ fewer positive pixels ($pos\_frac=0.0029$ vs.\ $0.0205$),
pulling the random AUC-PR floor down by the same factor. The relevant
cross-distribution measure is the \emph{lift over random}, AUC-PR $/$
pos\_frac (Table~\ref{tab:region}). In lift units, all four cells sit
between $10$--$12\times$ with no measurable degradation off the diagonal,
evidence that PI-GNODE has learned a generalizable spread dynamic rather
than a regime-specific shortcut. Notably, the high-elev diagonal AUC-PR
of 0.249 exceeds the full-data result (0.221, Table~\ref{tab:main}),
consistent with the denser, more deterministic slope-driven spread
patterns of mountainous fires.

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis}\label{sec:qualitative}

PI-GNODE produces smooth, well-localized probability maps that respect
the previous-day fire as a strict lower bound (eq.~\ref{eq:mono}), with
predicted spread concentrated in a narrow halo around the perimeter. The
characteristic failure mode is under-prediction of fast-spreading fires:
PI-GNODE captures perimeter direction but underestimates extent. See
Appendix~\ref{app:qualitative} for prediction maps on the four
highest-fire-content test events.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}\label{sec:conclusion}

PI-GNODE is competitive with discrete GNN baselines on the Next Day
Wildfire Spread benchmark while offering an inference-time monotonicity
guarantee and a continuous-time interpretation of message passing. Its
$\sim$11$\times$ lift-over-random performance is consistent across an
elevation-based cross-region generalization split, evidence that the
learned dynamics transfer cleanly across mountainous and lowland fire
regimes. Three directions extend this work:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
\item \textbf{Hybrid CNN$+$GNN.} The Convolutional Autoencoder's lead on
the headline benchmark suggests multi-scale spatial priors complement
message passing; a U-Net encoder feeding into PI-GNODE's ODE could combine
both inductive biases.
\item \textbf{Multi-day rollout on real multi-day data.} The continuous-time
formulation extends naturally to longer horizons, but NDWS only provides
$t \to t{+}1$ supervision. A multi-day benchmark such as
TS-SatFire~\cite{tssatfire2025} would enable direct evaluation of
multi-step rollout (with teacher forcing as a fallback at day boundaries).
\item \textbf{Learnable edge-feature normalization.} Our negative result
on hand-crafted edges may reverse with a learned LayerNorm on edge
attributes, addressing the scale-heterogeneity hypothesis directly.
\end{itemize}
